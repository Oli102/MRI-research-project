Research Notes & Sources

Johns Hopkins Medicine (Article)

  • Link: https://www.hopkinsmedicine.org/health/treatment-tests-and-therapies/magnetic-resonance-imaging-mri
  • How it was useful: Provided the patient's perspective, specialty scans, and why MRI is chosen over a CT scan.
  • Quick Notes: Best for seeing soft tissues. Patients must remove all metal, lie perfectly still, and wear earplugs to block loud magnetic knocking noises.

NIH (Article)

  • Link: https://www.nibib.nih.gov/science-education/science-topics/magnetic-resonance-imaging-mri
  • How it was useful: Simple explanation of how it works, risks, and new MRI tech developments.
  • Quick Notes: A massive magnet aligns hydrogen protons, a radio wave knocks them out of alignment, and they release a signal when snapping back. Dangerous for patients with metal implants.

NIST (Article)

  • Link: https://www.nist.gov/how-do-you-measure-it/how-does-mri-machine-work
  • How it was useful: In-depth, technical explanation of magnetic gradients interacting with protons.
  • Quick Notes: Uses 3 smaller gradient magnets to alter the magnetic field, assigning unique frequencies to different body parts so the computer can build a 3D image like GPS coordinates.

Johns Hopkins Medicine (YouTube Video)

  • Link: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jLnuPKhKXVM
  • How it was useful: Visual representation of atomic spin, proton disruption, and T1/T2 tissues.
  • Quick Notes: Showed how the RF pulse pushes protons against the main magnet, and how T1/T2 relaxation times determine how bright fluids or fats look on the screen.
